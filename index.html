Action research is problem-centered, client-centered, and action-oriented. It involves the client system, and a change agent involved in conducting a diagnostic, active-learning, problem-finding, and problem-solving process. The client and the change agent collaborate to identify and prioritize specific problems, analyze data to find the root causes, and develop action plans for coping with them realistically and practically. (Institute of Organizational Development, 2019)

Action Research (AR) is an approach to enquiry which has been used since the 1940s. It involves researchers and participants working together to understand a problematic situation and change it for the better. There are many definitions of the approach, which share some common elements. AR focuses on social change that promotes democracy and challenges inequality; is context-specific, often targeted on the needs of a particular group; is an iterative cycle of research, action, and reflection; and often seeks to ‘liberate’ participants to have a greater awareness of their situation in order to take action. AR uses a range of different methods, both qualitative and quantitative.

Participatory approaches to research, action research, and evaluation are based on similar philosophies, theories, and methods. For example, they start with many of the same underlying assumptions, such as:

  • People don’t need advanced degrees or professional credentials to conduct valuable research.
  • All groups and cultures have their own biases, including professional researchers and evaluators who are trying to remain “neutral” or “objective” observers.
  • Everyone can contribute valuable expertise, insights, and knowledge to a research or evaluation process.
  • Those who are closest to an issue, problem, or program generally know the most about it.
  • The involvement of diverse participants with different perspectives can help researchers, evaluators, practitioners, and community members produce insights that are less biased and closer to the truth.

1. Introduction to the Problem

Start by providing a detailed description of the problem or issue you're investigating. Explain the context in which the problem exists, including any relevant historical, social, or organizational background.

Example: If your action research focuses on improving student engagement in a classroom, describe the current state of student engagement, any trends or patterns you've observed, and the broader educational context.

2. Relevant Literature

Include a review of existing research related to your problem. Summarize key findings from previous studies to show that the issue is recognized and studied within the field.

Citation Example: Smith (2020) found that student engagement significantly impacts academic performance, particularly in high school settings.

3. Current Practices and Limitations

Discuss current practices or interventions related to the problem. Highlight their strengths and limitations based on your observations or existing research.

Citation Example: According to Jones and Lee (2018), current strategies for increasing classroom engagement often fail to address individual learning needs.

4. Specific Context

Detail the specific setting of your research (e.g., the particular school or classroom). Explain any unique characteristics of this setting that make it relevant to your study.

Citation Example: The school in this study has a diverse student population, which aligns with the findings of Garcia et al. (2019) on diverse educational settings.

Rationale

1. Justification for the Study

Articulate why your action research is necessary. Explain how addressing this problem will benefit the setting or community you're working with.

Citation Example: As highlighted by Williams (2021), targeted interventions are essential for improving educational outcomes in underserved communities.

2. Research Gaps

Identify gaps in existing research or practice that your study aims to fill. Demonstrate how your research will contribute new insights or solutions.

Citation Example: While previous studies such as those by Brown and Patel (2017) have explored general engagement strategies, few have examined their effectiveness in small-group settings.

3. Potential Impact

Discuss the potential impact of your research findings. Explain how the results could influence practice, policy, or future research.

Citation Example: Effective strategies for enhancing engagement could lead to improved academic outcomes, as suggested by Clark et al. (2022).

Writing a Literature Review in APA 7th Edition

Evaluating the Literature Review

  • Extensive? A broad and comprehensive survey?
  • Current? Up to date, state of the art?
  • Relevant? Closely related to and well-focused on the topic and the research problem you are investigating?
  • Thorough? An in-depth survey of the major contributions of significant studies to the body of knowledge under review, identifying trends, evidence, and conclusions in the relevant literature?
  • Critical? A critical comparison of different approaches that highlights methodological flaws or gaps, inconsistencies in theory and findings, and areas or issues needing further study?

Approaches in APA 7th Edition Manual of Style

1. End-of-text References

An example of a journal article end-of-text reference in APA form is:

Elam, S. M. (1989). The second Phi Delta Kappa poll of teachers’ attitudes toward public schools. Phi Delta Kappan, 70(3), 785–798.

An example of a book end-of-text reference in APA form is:

Shertzer, B., & Stone, S. C. (1981). Fundamentals of guidance (4th ed.). Boston: Houghton Mifflin.

An example of a conference paper end-of-text reference in APA form is:

Zedexk, S., & Baker, H. T. (1971, May). Evaluation of behavioral expectation scales. Paper presented.

The words “Retrieved from” precede the URL name as in the following example of a journal article reference:

Smith, J. (2008, June). Sciences vs. ideologies. Science, 29(3). Retrieved from http://www.apa.org/monitor/.

The DOI number is typically located on the first page of the electronic journal article. When cited, this number is placed at the end of a reference, as shown in the following example:

Smith, J. P. (2005). Mixed methods research: Its controversies and potential. Journal of Mixed Methods Research, 3(1), 34–50. doi: 1038/0278.6133.24.2.226

2. Within-text References

An example of a within-text reference in APA style where the author refers to a single reference:

Rogers (1994) compared reaction times for athletes and nonathletes in middle schools...

An example of a within-text reference in APA style where the author refers to multiple references:

Past studies of reaction times (Gogel, 1984; Rogers, 1994; Smith, 1989) showed...

When a work has only two authors, you should cite both names every time the reference occurs in the text:

Kurines and Smith (2009) demonstrated...

3. Headings

  • Headings provide important clues for readers that help them understand a study.
  • They divide the material like topics in an outline.
  • In APA style, the maximum number of heading levels is five.
  • Most educational studies include either two or three levels of headings.
  • Authors seldom use fourth- or fifth-level headings unless their research requires many subdivisions.

Writing Reviews of Literature Strategies

1. Extent of the Review

There is no easy answer to how long a literature review should be, but you can begin by considering the type of research report you are writing.

  • Dissertations and Theses: Require an extensive review of the literature, often including all identified sources of information.
  • Research Plans or Proposals: A less comprehensive literature review may suffice, though guidelines may be set by advisors or reviewers.
  • Research Proposals and Journal Articles: Focus on studies from the last 10 years, with exceptions for classical studies that substantially influence the literature.

2. Thematic Review of the Literature

In a thematic review, the researcher identifies a theme and briefly cites literature to document it. This approach discusses only the major ideas or results from studies.

This approach is common in journal articles but also used in dissertations and theses. It identifies themes and references the literature used to support them.

3. Study-by-Study Review of Literature

This approach provides a detailed summary of each study, grouped under broad themes. The summaries include elements of an abstract and are linked by transitional sentences under subheadings that reflect themes and major divisions.

Writing RRL Critically

Critical reviews of literature involve analytical persuasive writing, which evaluates the work of other researchers.

  • Critically reviewing the literature moves from description to critique by linking an evaluative comment to the information.
  • The focus of the comment is often on the research topic rather than the researcher.
  • Such critique may be positive or negative.

Aspects to Critique in Literature Reviews:

  • The conclusions or implications drawn by a researcher because of a study.
  • The methodology of a research study.
  • The theoretical framework used.
  • The validity and/or reliability of the study.
  • The data obtained in a study.

Theoretical frameworks largely work at the time when a theoretical roadmap has been laid about a certain topic and the research being undertaken by the researcher carefully analyzes it, and works on similar lines to attain successful results.

  • The research question lies at the heart of every piece of academic writing. The research question determines the sources to be quoted, how to structure the argument, and what a paper aims at.
  • The research question narrows down the topic and makes sure that the paper has a common thread. Moreover, the research question gives the reader a clear idea of what to expect from the paper.
  • You cannot start writing without having a research question in mind. It is easiest to think of a research question as a Wh-question. Finding an answer to the research question you formulate is your personal contribution. You must state your research question right at the beginning, as part of the introduction.
Strong Research Questions (Combes, 2019)

• A good research question is essential to guide your research paper, project or thesis. It pinpoints exactly what you want to find out and gives your work a clear focus and purpose. All research questions should be:

  • Focused on a single problem or issue
  • Researchable using primary and/or secondary sources
  • Feasible to answer within the timeframe and practical constraints
  • Specific enough to answer thoroughly
  • Complex enough to develop the answer over the space of a paper or thesis
  • Relevant to your field of study and/or society more broadly

  • According to McLeod (2021) A hypothesis (plural hypotheses) is a precise, testable statement of what the researcher(s) predict will be the outcome of the study. It is stated at the start of the study.
  • This usually involves proposing a possible relationship between two variables: the independent variable (what the researcher changes) and the dependent variable (what the research measures).
  • In research, there is a convention that the hypothesis is written in two forms, the null hypothesis, and the alternative hypothesis (called the experimental hypothesis when the method of investigation is an experiment).
Alternative Hypothesis (Ha) Null Hypothesis (Ho)
  1. Ha states that there is a relationship between two variables being studied or one variable influences the other.
  2. An experimental hypothesis predicts what changes will take place in the DV when the IV is manipulated.
  3. Ha states that the results are not due to change and that they are significant in terms of supporting the theory being investigated.
  1. Ho states that there is no relationship between the two variables being studied or one variable does not affect the other.
  2. There will be no changes in the DV due to the manipulation of the independent variable.
  3. Ho states results are due to chance and are not significant in terms of supporting the idea being investigated.
A research hypothesis must be specific and clear, testable and falsifiable?
Examples:

Ha: Increasing instructional time in Math will result in decreasing frequency of non-numerates.

Ho: Increasing instructional time in Math will have no effect on frequency of non-numerates.

Ha: Teachers who have 6 hours working time reports greater job satisfaction than teachers who work 8 hours.

Ho: There is no relationship between 6 hours working time to job satisfaction.

The significance of the study presents the importance of your research. It gives you an opportunity to prove the study’s impact on your field of endeavor, the knowledge it contributes, and the people who will benefit from it. It is a written explanation and justification of the importance of the study. In writing the significance of the study please bear in mind the following:

Scope and delimitation are two elements of a research paper. It identifies what topic the research intends to cover. The scope names the information or subject matter that the individual plans to evaluate. However, it is not possible for a researcher to cover varied aspects of of a chosen subject. In this case, the scope can be narrowed down to a manageable topic or concentration known as delimitations. It defines factors the researcher consciously controls for and why they have been eliminated from the study.

In action research, sampling techniques are crucial for collecting data that is representative and relevant to the research question. Here are some common sampling techniques used in action research:

  1. Purposive Sampling: This technique involves selecting participants who are most relevant to the research question. Researchers deliberately choose individuals who have specific characteristics or experiences related to the study. This is particularly useful in action research where the focus is on specific groups or situations.
  2. Convenience Sampling: This method involves selecting participants who are easiest to reach and available at the time of the study. While this technique is often used due to its practicality, it may not always provide the most representative sample.
  3. Snowball Sampling: This technique is used when the population of interest is hard to access. Initial participants are asked to refer others who meet the study criteria. This method is particularly useful for reaching out to specific or hard-to-reach groups.
  4. Stratified Sampling: In this approach, the population is divided into subgroups or strata based on certain characteristics (e.g., age, gender, socioeconomic status), and then participants are randomly selected from each stratum. This technique ensures that different subgroups are represented in the sample.
  5. Thematic Sampling: This is used when researchers want to focus on particular themes or issues. Participants are chosen based on their ability to provide insights into these themes. It’s particularly useful in qualitative action research where the goal is to understand specific aspects of a phenomenon.
  6. Systematic Sampling: Researchers select every nth individual from a list or population. For example, every 10th person on a list might be chosen. This technique can be useful when there is a clear, ordered list of potential participants.
  7. Quota Sampling: Similar to stratified sampling, quota sampling involves dividing the population into subgroups and then selecting participants from each subgroup to meet a specific quota. Unlike stratified sampling, participants are not randomly chosen, but the method ensures representation of key subgroups.

• Data analysis is a process that involves examining, and molding collected data for interpretation to discover relevant information, draw or propose conclusions and support decision-making to solve a research problem. This involves interpreting data to answer research questions and making research findings be ready for dissemination. Data analysis also serves as a reference for future data collection and other research activities. During data analysis (Bala, 2005):

  • data collected is transformed into information and knowledge about research performed
  • relationships between variables are explored
  • meanings are identified and information is interpreted.
  • Like other research methods, data analysis procedures in quantitative research approach are different from those in qualitative research approach. The general differences of data analysis procedures between these two approaches are summarized in the Chart (Bala, 2005)

• Data analysis is the most crucial part of any research. Data analysis summarizes collected data. It involves the interpretation of data gathered using analytical and logical reasoning to determine patterns, relationships or trends. (Ancho, 2023)

The usual step proceeding data analysis is interpretation. “Interpretation involves attaching meaning and significance to the analysis, explaining descriptive patterns, and looking for relationships and linkages among descriptive dimensions. Once these processes have been completed the researcher must report his or her interpretations and conclusions” (Krueger, 1994).

Steps in interpreting data

Keep your research question in mind: The most important piece of advice for interpreting the results is to keep your research question in mind. Your interpretation should be centered on addressing your research question, and all your analysis should be directed in that direction.

Consider alternate explanations: It’s critical to think about alternative explanations for your results. Ask yourself whether any other circumstances might be impacting your findings, and carefully assess them. This can assist guarantee that your interpretation is based on the evidence and not on assumptions or biases.

Contextualize the results: Put the results into perspective by comparing them to past research in the topic at hand. This can assist in identifying trends, patterns, or discrepancies that you may have missed otherwise, as well as providing a foundation for subsequent research.

Research Dissemination Types

There are many options for researchers to get their findings out to the world. The type of desired dissemination plays a big role in choosing the medium and the tone to take when sharing the information. Some common types include:

  • Academic dissemination: Sharing research findings in academic journals, which typically involves a peer-review process.
  • Policy-oriented dissemination: Creating documents that summarize research findings in a way that's understandable to policymakers.
  • Public dissemination: Using television and other media outlets to communicate research findings to the public.
  • Educational dissemination: Developing curricula for education settings that incorporate research findings.
  • Digital and online dissemination: Using digital platforms to present research findings to a global audience.
  • Strategic business presentation: Creating a presentation for a business group to use research insights to shape business strategy.
